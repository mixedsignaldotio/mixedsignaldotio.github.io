Energy Bands in Solids

This article introduces the foundations of energy band theory in solids, beginning with atomic structure and discrete energy levels, then extending to energy bands in crystals. It explains how band gaps classify materials into insulators, semiconductors, or metals, highlighting the roles of electrons, holes, and quantum mechanics in conduction.

By Mr. Amitabh Yadav Ph.D. Candidate, École Polytechnique Fédérale de Lausanne (EPFL)
Share:

In isolated atoms, electrons occupy discrete energy levels. When many atoms bind into a periodic crystal, the overlap of outer (valence) orbitals causes each atomic level to split into an enormous number of closely spaced levels. These form quasi-continuous energy bands, which govern electrical conduction.

The chapter "Energy Bands in Solids" begins with the fundamental properties of charged particles. The electron has charge $$q = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}\, C$$ and mass $$m = 9.11 \times 10^{-31}\, kg.$$ Since one coulomb corresponds to about $$6 \times 10^{18}$$ electrons, even picoampere currents represent millions of electrons per second. In crystals like silicon, atoms form covalent bonds by sharing electrons. If an electron is missing, the vacancy behaves as a "hole," a positive charge carrier.

The electric field intensity $\mathcal{E}$ relates force and charge: $$f = q\mathcal{E} = m\frac{dv}{dt} \quad (1-1)$$ Potential is defined as: $$\mathcal{E} = -\frac{dV}{dx} \quad (1-2, 1-3)$$ Potential energy is: $$U = qV \quad (1-4)$$ and the total energy is conserved: $$W = U + \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 = \text{constant} \quad (1-5)$$

The electron-volt (eV) is a natural unit of energy in semiconductor physics: $$1\, eV = 1.60 \times 10^{-19}\, J \quad (1-7)$$ It is the energy gained by an electron accelerated through 1 volt.

Rutherford’s model depicted electrons orbiting a dense positive nucleus, but classical mechanics predicted instability. Bohr resolved this by postulating discrete stationary states. Electrons radiate only during transitions between levels: $$f = \frac{W_2 - W_1}{h} \quad (1-11)$$ with quantized angular momentum: $$mvr = \frac{nh}{2\pi} \quad (1-12)$$ This yields hydrogen atom energies: $$W = -\frac{mq^4}{8h^2\epsilon_0^2 n^2} \quad (1-13)$$ with ionization energy of 13.6 eV.

Electrons can be excited by collisions or photons. A photon has energy: $$E = hf \quad (1-14)$$ and de Broglie related matter waves to momentum: $$\lambda = \frac{h}{mv} \quad (1-15)$$ Thus, electrons exhibit both particle and wave properties. Schrödinger’s wave mechanics confirmed quantized solutions consistent with Bohr’s model.

In multi-electron atoms, quantum numbers $n$, $l$, $m_l$, and $m_s$ define electron states, and Pauli’s exclusion principle prohibits two electrons from sharing identical quantum states. Atoms organize into shells (K, L, M, N) and subshells (s, p, d, f). Group IVA elements (C, Si, Ge, Sn) are tetravalent, contributing four valence electrons, but exhibit different solid-state properties depending on band formation.

When atoms form crystals, inner electrons remain localized, but outer electrons overlap significantly. For $N$ atoms, each orbital produces $N$ nearly degenerate states. These broaden into continuous energy bands. Two critical bands form: - The Valence Band, filled with bonding electrons. - The Conduction Band, higher energy states available for conduction. Between them lies the Forbidden Energy Gap: $$E_g = E_c - E_v$$

Classification of solids follows from $E_g$: - Insulators: Wide gap, $E_g > 5 \, eV$, e.g., diamond ($E_g \approx 6 eV$). No conduction under normal conditions. - Semiconductors: Narrow gap, $E_g \sim 0.5 - 1.5 \, eV$, e.g., silicon ($1.21 eV$), germanium ($0.785 eV$). Thermal excitation produces electron-hole pairs, increasing conductivity with temperature. - Metals: Overlapping bands or partially filled conduction band. Always conductive, even at 0 K.

Holes in the valence band act as positive charge carriers and contribute to current flow alongside electrons. Doping with impurities introduces new energy levels inside the forbidden gap, creating extrinsic semiconductors. Intrinsic semiconductors rely on pure thermal excitation, while extrinsic conduction is dominated by dopants.

Finally, the band gap is temperature-dependent: as interatomic spacing changes, $E_g$ decreases with increasing temperature, further increasing conductivity. Thus, band theory unifies atomic structure, quantum mechanics, and solid-state physics to explain why materials act as insulators, semiconductors, or conductors.
